Xinchang Wang is an Associate Professor of Operations Management in the Department of Finance & Management Science at the Carson College of Business, Washington State University. He joined Washington State University as an Assistant Professor in 2019 and was promoted to Associate Professor with tenure in 2025. Prior to joining Washington State University, Dr. Wang was an Assistant Professor of Business Quantitative Analysis in the College of Business at Mississippi State University from 2015 to 2019.

Xinchang received a Ph.D. in Operations Research from the Georgia Institute of Technology in 2015 and a Ph.D. in Civil and Environmental Engineering from the National University of Singapore in 2011, with a specialization in transportation and logistics.

Xinchang’s research centers on investigating operations problems (e.g., pricing and revenue management, capacity sizing, discrete choice modeling, and demand forecasting) in service systems, spanning queueing systems, port and terminal systems, transportation, and supply chains, through the development of both modeling-based and data-driven methods and algorithms.

Papers under Review

  1. Kazemi, M. S., Wang, X. 2025. Revenue maximization for seaport terminal operations considering multimodal cargo flows.

    Abstract

    Introduction: This work aims to determine optimal prices that a seaport terminal operator quotes to cargo suppliers/shippers who ship cargoes to the port via different modes of tranport, such as trucking, rail, inland waterways and airfreight, and desire their cargoes to be processed at the port. The prolem is formulated as a non-convex, nonlinear mathematical program with equilibrium constraints, in which a Nested Logit model is employed to capture the hierarchical mode-first, route-second choice behavior of cargo suppliers. Analytical Results: By a change-of-variable method, the model is transformed into flow-based models, enabling us to develop lightweight optimality equations with a reduced number of variables. We develop efficient search algorithms to solve the model by leveraging these optimality equations. Applications: Our algorithms are compared with a wide range of benchmarking algorithms in numerical studies, and the findings demonstrate the efficiency of the developed algorithms.

    Abstract

    Introduction: This work investigates whether a service provider is better off making a single-server queue observable or unobservable under optimal dynamic pricing.   2. Wang, X.*, Xu, W.#, 2025. Joint estimation of the arrival rate and customer taste coefficients from censored transactional data. Production and Operations Management, 34(9), pp. 2853-2872.

    Abstract

    Introduction: This work focuses on demand forecasting from incomplete, censored transactional data, where lost sales are not recorded. The problem is formulated as a maximum likelihood estimation problem, in which our goal is to jointly estimate the arrival rates and hierarchical choice behavior of customers under a Nested Logit model. Analytical Results: We characterize necessary and sufficient identifiability conditions under which the model parameters can be uniquely determined. We also develop sequential majorization-minimization algorithms for estimating the model parameters and show their convergence under data-related conditions. Applications: Through numerical studies, we compare our algorithms with benchmark methods in demand-forecasting settings for an airline and an online retailing platform.

  3. Wang, X., He, Q., 2023. Optimal capacity sizing of park-and-ride lots with information-aware commuters. Production and Operations Management, 32(11), pp. 3614-3633.

    Abstract

    Introduction: This work focuses on determining the capacities of park-and-ride lots for information-aware commuters who can access the utilization information of all available lots and choose which lot to visit based on that information and road congestion. The problem is formulated as a nonlinear, nonconvex mathematical program with equilibrium constraints that capture the interaction between road traffic flows and commuter parking lot choice. Analytical Results: We show that except for at most one lot, the optimal capacity of each lot takes one of three possible values: the lower bound on capacity, the upper bound on capacity, or the equilibrium traffic flow. Applications: In a numerical example for seven park-and-ride lots in Bellevue, Washington, the results suggest that a large lot close to downtown can be desirable when budget and space allow. However, the lot may still have a low utilization rate because congestion can cause long bus waiting times.

  5. Suk, T., Wang, X.*, 2021. Optimal pricing policies for tandem queues: Asymptotic optimality. IISE Transactions, 53(2), pp. 199-220.

    Abstract

    Introduction: This work studies optimal pricing for a tandem line with possibly many stations connected in series. The objective is to maximize the long-run average revenue of the service provider. Analytical Results: We show that the optimal static pricing policies perform as well as the optimal dynamic pricing policies when the buffer before station 1 becomes large and the arrival rate is either small or large. We also propose two specific static pricing policies and show that each policy produces a gain converging to the optimal gain at an approximately exponential rate as the buffer before station 1 becomes large. Applications: Through numerical studies, we find that the proposed static policies perform as well as optimal dynamic policies even for a moderate-sized buffer before station 1. We also find cases where optimal static pricing policies are neither optimal nor near-optimal.

  6. Wang, X., Andradottir, S., Ayhan, H., Suk, T., 2020. Revenue maximization in two-station tandem queueing systems. Naval Research Logistics, 67(2), pp. 77-107.

    Abstract

    Introduction: This work studies optimal pricing for a tandem line with two stations connected in sequence. The objective is to maximize the long-run average revenue of the service provider. Analytical Results: For systems with two stations, general-sized buffers, and two or more prices, we describe the structure of the optimal dynamic pricing policy and develop tailored policy-iteration algorithms to find an optimal policy. For systems with two stations but no intermediate buffer, we characterize conditions under which quoting either a high price or a low price to all customers is optimal and provide an easy-to-implement algorithm to solve the problem. Applications: Through numerical studies, we find that the proposed tailored policy-iteration algorithm outperforms the regular policy-iteration algorithm. Extensions: This work also discusses possible extensions to three-station systems and to two-station systems with price- and congestion-sensitive customers.

  7. Wang, X., Andradottir, S., Ayhan, H., 2019. Optimal pricing for tandem queues with finite buffers. Queueing Systems: Theory and Applications, 92(3-4), pp. 323-396.

    Abstract

    Introduction: This work studies optimal pricing for a tandem queueing system with two stations, a finite buffer in front of each station, and incoming customers who are sensitive to the quoted price. The objective is to maximize both the long-run average revenue and the infinite-horizon discounted revenue of the service provider. Analytical Results: We show that, under certain conditions on the holding costs, there exists an optimal dynamic policy with a monotone structure, in which the quoted price is non-decreasing in the queue length at either station and non-increasing when a customer moves from station 1 to station 2. We also show that, in the long-run average problem, the optimal static policy performs as well as the optimal dynamic policy when the buffer before station 1 becomes large. Applications: Through numerical studies, we find that for systems with small arrival rates and no holding cost, the optimal static policy produces a gain close to the optimal gain even when the buffer before station 1 is small. On the other hand, when arrival rates are not small, the optimal dynamic policy can perform much better than the optimal static policy.

  8. Wang, X., Meng, Q., 2019. Optimal price decisions for joint ventures between port operators and shipping lines under the congestion effect. European Journal of Operational Research, 273(2), pp. 695-707.

    Abstract

    Introduction: This work addresses the optimal pricing problem for a firm operating a joint-venture terminal under the congestion effect. The firm is formed between a port terminal operator and a shipping line and provides a bundle of ocean transportation and port terminal solutions to cargo suppliers. Analytical Results: We develop a tractable flow-based optimization model that uses a fixed-point equation to capture the interaction between the congestion effect and cargo suppliers' choice. We characterize the optimal solution for the single origin-destination case, the partially homogeneous case, and the heterogeneous case. We propose one-variable and two-variable search methods to solve the model, by which the original problem is reduced to solving simple nonliner equations with one or two variables. Applications: Through numerical studies, we evaluate the profit loss incurred by ignoring the congestion effect. We find that the firm should quote the same price to all cargo suppliers under the fully homogeneous case, but this is not necessarily optimal under the partially homogeneous or heterogeneous cases.

  10. Wang, X. 2017. Static and dynamic resource allocation models for single-leg transportation markets with service disruptions. Transportation Research Part E: Logistics and Transportation Review, 103, pp. 87-108.

    Abstract

  18. Wang, X., Andradottir, S., Ayhan, H., 2015. Dynamic server assignment with task-dependent server synergy. IEEE Transactions on Automatic Control, 60(2), pp. 570-575.

    Abstract

    Introduction: We study tandem queueing systems with finite buffers in which servers work more efficiently in teams than on their own and the synergy among collaborating servers can be task dependent. Our goal is to determine the dynamic server assignment policy that maximizes the long-run average throughput. Analytical Results: When each server works with the same ability at each task that she/he is assigned to, we show that any nonidling policy where all servers work in teams of two or more at all times is optimal. On the other hand, when the server abilities are task-dependent, we show that for Markovian systems with two stations and two servers, depending on the synergy among the servers, the optimal policy either assigns the two servers to different stations when possible, or lets them work in a team at all times. Applications: This work can be applicable to manufacturing, telecommunication, and service systems.

  19. Meng, Q., Wang, X., 2011. Intermodal hub-and-spoke network design: Incorporating multiple stakeholders and multi-type containers. Transportation Research Part B: Methodological, 45(4), pp. 724-742.

    Abstract

    Introduction: This paper develops a mathematical program with equilibrium constraints model for the intermodal hub-and-spoke network design problem with multiple stakeholders and multi-type containers. Analytical Results: The model incorporates a parametric variational inequality that formulates the user equilibrium behavior of intermodal operators in route choice for any given network design decision, and it uses a cost function that reflects the transition from scale economies to scale diseconomies in distinct flow regimes for carriers and hub operators. Applications: Comparative analysis against an exhaustive enumeration algorithm indicates good performance of the developed algorithm in computational time and solution quality.

    Abstract

    Introduction: Urban road tunnels provide an increasingly cost-effective engineering solution, especially in compact cities such as Singapore.
